# Referee Notes and Stress Tests

## Core Contribution
This paper shows that introducing stablecoins in a fixed exchange rate regime:
1. Improves information (public signal)
2. Increases coordination (global games)
3. Raises crisis risk
4. Generates non-monotonic welfare effects

## What a Referee Would Ask

### 1. Is this just better signal → more runs?
Response:
- Yes, but the key novelty is embedding this in a **parallel market microstructure**
- Stablecoins are not just signals; they are **traded assets** that affect equilibrium quantities

### 2. Are results calibration-driven?
Response:
- Core results (monotonic demand, crisis amplification, welfare sign switch) are robust across calibrations
- Magnitudes vary, but signs are stable

### 3. Why not just one asset?
Response:
- Two assets matter because:
  - different costs
  - different information content
  - different scalability

### 4. Is global games necessary?
Response:
- Yes: without it, no coordination amplification
- With it, small information improvements → large equilibrium effects

## Stress Tests to Highlight in Paper

- Low rho (weak signal effect): results weaken but persist
- High alpha (strong regime): crises disappear
- High delta (fragile regime): welfare losses dominate

## Recommended Framing

"Stablecoins act as a *high-frequency public signal embedded in a tradable asset*, altering both beliefs and coordination in parallel FX markets."
