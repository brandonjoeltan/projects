# Mock Referee Report and Response Strategy

## Referee Report (Mock)

### Summary
This paper studies how stablecoins affect parallel foreign exchange markets and fixed exchange rate fragility. The core idea is that stablecoins are not just cheaper transaction devices, but also generate a public signal that strengthens coordination in a speculative environment. The paper combines a hedge-demand margin with a global-games run margin and shows that stablecoins can both improve access to foreign currency and increase crisis risk. The main welfare result is non-monotonicity: stablecoins are beneficial at low exchange-rate misalignment but harmful when misalignment is sufficiently severe.

The paper is interesting and potentially important. The mechanism is novel and policy-relevant. The model is richer than a standard speculative attack framework because the public signal is endogenous and tied to a traded instrument. The paper also connects naturally to current debates on digital currencies and exchange-rate management.

At the same time, the paper would benefit from sharpening its contribution and clarifying the quantitative strategy. Below I list the main strengths and concerns.

### Main strengths
1. The central mechanism is original and important: stablecoins act as a public signal embedded in a tradable asset.
2. The combination of a hedge motive and a coordination motive is economically sensible.
3. The paper speaks to macro-financial stability in an environment that is increasingly relevant in practice.
4. The welfare tradeoff is non-trivial and policy-relevant.

### Main concerns

#### 1. Is the contribution just "better public information increases runs"?
The paper should make clearer what is genuinely new relative to standard global-games models with public information. In its current form, some readers may see the contribution as an application rather than a new insight.

#### 2. Why are two assets necessary?
The paper needs to clarify why the distinction between cash foreign currency and stablecoins is essential. Could the same results be obtained in a one-asset model with better information?

#### 3. Quantitative strategy needs clearer hierarchy
The paper uses both a full solver and a fast approximation. The paper should be explicit about which one is the benchmark and which one is used for screening and calibration.

#### 4. Welfare magnitudes appear calibration-sensitive
The sign of the welfare result seems robust, but magnitudes depend on parameter choices. The paper should present this clearly and avoid overclaiming quantitative precision.

#### 5. More discussion of realism would help
The reader would benefit from a brief discussion of why the chosen crisis probabilities and welfare magnitudes are empirically plausible.

---

## Response Strategy

### Response to Concern 1
The paper should emphasize that the public signal is endogenous and generated by equilibrium demand for a tradable asset. This differs from standard global-games environments where public information is exogenous. The key sentence should be:

> Stablecoins act as a high-frequency public signal embedded in a tradable asset, so financial innovation changes both transaction technology and the information structure of the parallel market.

### Response to Concern 2
The paper should state explicitly that two assets are necessary because the paper seeks to separate:
- the transaction-cost channel
- the information/coordination channel

A one-asset model would confound these margins.

### Response to Concern 3
The paper should clearly state:
- the exact / fully nested solver is the benchmark model implementation
- the fast solver preserves the same economics and is used for screening and tractable calibration search

### Response to Concern 4
The paper should describe the welfare result as robust in sign but calibration-sensitive in magnitude. This is a strength, not a weakness, as it highlights the importance of baseline regime fragility.

### Response to Concern 5
The quantitative section should explicitly note that the preferred calibration aims for moderate crisis probabilities and a mild welfare sign switch, to match plausible macro-financial environments rather than extreme collapse scenarios.

---

## Recommended revisions to the paper
1. Tighten the introduction around the endogenous public-signal contribution.
2. Add one paragraph in the model section explaining why two assets are necessary.
3. Add one paragraph in the quantitative section clarifying the solver hierarchy.
4. Present the welfare result as a robust qualitative tradeoff with calibration-dependent magnitudes.
5. Add a brief robustness subsection distinguishing the mild baseline calibration from a more fragile alternative calibration.
